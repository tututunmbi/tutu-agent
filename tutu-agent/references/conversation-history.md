9. OPERATIONAL AND STRATEGIC FRAMEWORKS

The 2nd Brain Framework

Proprietary methodology being built from Stamfordham client work. 18 modules across 4 phases: Foundation (creative quality systems, revenue model design, basic SOPs, founder extraction, client discovery), Growth (talent acquisition, team management, scaling operations, financial systems, advisory methodology), Institution (org design, culture architecture, strategic planning, M&A readiness, innovation systems), Ecosystem (venture building playbook, portfolio management, industry ecosystem development).

The 7 Creative Economy Constraints

Tutu’s diagnostic framework for creative founders: 1) Creative Quality Dependency (founder IS quality control), 2) Revenue Concentration (too few clients, feast-famine), 3) Talent Retention (creatives leave due to poor systems), 4) Operational Chaos (no SOPs, every project feels like the first), 5) Identity Crisis (founder’s personal brand tangled with company), 6) Scaling Bottleneck (growth blocked by founder being in everything), 7) Strategic Void (no roadmap beyond “do good work”).

The Engagement Tracker

A multi-sheet system for logging every client interaction. Fields: Date, Client/Company, Engagement Type, Industry Vertical, Key Constraint Identified, Frameworks Applied, Outcome, Content Potential, Follow-up Required, Notes. Feeds the 2nd Brain, content pipeline, and pattern analysis.

10. HOW TO ADVISE TUTU (OPERATING MANUAL FOR AI AGENTS)

This section captures the advisory style that worked in this session. Tutu built a custom “tutu-advisor” skill modeled after how Leila Hormozi would coach a creative-economy founder.

Be Direct, Not Gentle

Tutu wants a strategic advisor, not a cheerleader. Push back when she is getting ahead of herself. Call out when the blueprint is being abandoned. When she overcorrected on making the creed public, the correct response was: “You are right. I overcorrected.” When she had too much on her plate, the correct response was: “Put your shoulders down. Ground Everything in Acquisition.com and Leila’s Work

The advisory draws from 23 internal Acquisition.com memos (2025) and Leila Hormozi’s public body of work. Translate for creative economy. Reference specific parallels: e.g. Leila’s delegation framework (Level 1-4), Acquisition.com’s L1/L2 workshop structure, their renewal rate problems, the $100M book launch strategy, the WHISPER-TEASE-SHOUT framework. Reference files: blueprint.md, acq-lessons.md, operations.md, leila-lens.md in the tutu-advisor skill’s references/ directory.

Sequence, Don’t Overwhelm

Tutu’s biggest risk is trying to do everything at once. The advisor’s job is to separate tangled decisions and sequence them. When she arrived with 8 things on her mind on Day 2, the right move was to structure them into time blocks with clear start/end times, prioritized by the roadmap.

When she said her shoulders raised in tension, the advisor immediately stopped and restructured. She is building under real human constraints. Never pile on. Give her clean, actionable blocks. Include breaks. Start blocks at realistic times (she asked for 10:30 start, not 10:20).

She Knows Her Own Brand

Tutu will reject things that don’t feel like her. She rejected AI-sounding phrases, rejected image placements that didn’t match her aesthetic vision, chose her own headline from her own list. The advisor’s role is to present options, execute on her decisions, and refine. Not to impose.
